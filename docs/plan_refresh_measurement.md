# Plan: measuring the real board's refresh rate defensibly

Status: plan, 2026-07-12. Open question from ACCURACY.md: MAME sets
60 Hz with an "Unknown/Unverified" comment; our core derives 60.01 Hz
(pixel clock 26.6660/4 MHz over 424 x 262 totals). Nobody has measured
the real board. Two independent original-PCB recordings are in hand
(reference/hyprduel_1cc_pcb.mp4 and a second capture, different
uploader/chain), which is what makes a defensible answer possible.

## Why this is not trivial

A YouTube video's container says "60 fps" but sits at the end of a
chain (capture card clock, encoder, YouTube re-encode) whose absolute
timebase cannot be trusted to the precision we need. Any method that
counts game frames against container frames measures the RATIO of the
board's rate to the chain's rate - the chain's absolute error is the
dominant systematic. The plan therefore anchors the timebase to
something we can verify independently (method C), uses two content
methods that fail differently (A, B), and requires agreement across
two independent recordings (D). A claim survives only if all four
agree within stated error bars.

## Method A: cumulative scroll slope (primary)

In a constant-velocity scroll segment (long stage stretch, no
slowdown), the background advances a fixed amount per GAME frame -
a rational constant set by game code. Measure per-container-frame
displacement by cross-correlating consecutive frames' background
strips (sub-pixel via parabolic interpolation of the correlation
peak), then fit cumulative displacement vs container frame index over
30+ seconds. Duplicated container frames appear as zero-steps
(staircase); fit the ramp, not the steps.

    slope [px/container-frame] = step [px/game-frame] x f_native / f_container

The game-code step is unknown but rational with a small denominator;
candidate steps are enumerated and the one placing f_native in a
physically plausible band (55-62 Hz) is selected - and cross-checked
in our own core, where the same scene's step is directly observable
in simulation.

Precision budget: over 1,800 frames and ~3,000 px of cumulative
travel, +-0.5 px total measurement error gives f to ~0.02%.

## Method B: duplicate-frame cadence (secondary)

If the board runs below the container rate, the capture must duplicate
one frame every 60/(60 - f_native) frames; the duplicate positions are
detectable as near-zero inter-frame difference. Count exact duplicate
positions over minutes of slowdown-free content:

    f_native = f_container x (1 - dups/frames)

Weaknesses (why it is secondary): game slowdown creates legitimate
repeats (must select segments with continuous motion), and a chain
that RESAMPLES rather than duplicates (blended frames) breaks the
detector. Used as a consistency check on A, not as the headline.

## Method C: audio-anchored timebase (the absolute reference)

The container's audio track shares the recording chain's clock. The
music tempo is generated by the YM2151's timer, clocked by the 4 MHz
crystal we verified from board photographs - so the true tempo of any
music passage is known exactly from our simulation (same ROM, same
timer code, crystal-exact clocking, previously verified against MAME
audio timing). Measuring the same passage's tempo in the video audio
gives the chain's absolute timebase error:

    chain_scale = tempo_measured_in_video / tempo_true

Applying chain_scale to methods A and B converts their ratios into
absolute Hz anchored to the photographed crystal. This closes the
"YouTube says 60 fps" hole.

## Method D: two independent recordings

All of A-C run on both captures (different players, different capture
hardware, different upload dates). Agreement within combined error
bars is the defensibility criterion. Disagreement means a chain
artefact and the result is NOT published.

## Interpretation

The pixel clock is fixed by the photographed 26.6660 MHz crystal
(divided by 4). The measured f_native selects the line/frame totals:

    H_TOTAL x V_TOTAL = 6,666,500 / f_native

Candidates: 424 x 262 = 111,088 -> 60.01 Hz (our current totals,
matching MAME's guess). A measurement near 58.3 Hz would imply
~114,350 total dots (e.g. 437 x 262 or 424 x 269-270) and our video
timing constants would be corrected accordingly - a real accuracy fix
affecting game speed and music-against-wallclock. A measurement at
60.01 +- error confirms the totals and upgrades MAME's guess to a
verified value, worth reporting upstream.

## Deliverables

1. tools/measure_refresh.py implementing A, B and the audio tempo
   comparison for C, with error propagation
2. Results table (both videos x three methods) in ACCURACY.md with the
   final value and uncertainty
3. If the answer differs from 60.01 Hz: H_TOTAL/V_TOTAL correction in
   rtl/i4220_vdp.sv, re-verified through the full ladder (the line
   BUDGET changes with H_TOTAL - the renderer margins must be
   re-measured)
4. Upstream note to MAME either way (their comment invites it)

## Field notes (2026-07-12, first implementation pass)

- Method A on stage-1: the star field scrolls sub-pixel per frame -
  too slow. Faster stages are slowdown-polluted, and slowdown is
  indistinguishable from cadence duplicates in aggregate: A needs a
  provably slowdown-free fast-scroll window (post-boss flyoffs are
  candidates).
- Method B: confirmed slowdown-dominated in this game; secondary only.
- Method C: naive beat extraction picks different beat multiples per
  source; needs onset-PATTERN time-scale matching (the OKI-pitch
  technique in the tempo domain) on the same tune.
- Method E (title blink): DEAD - Hyper Duel's PRESS START does not
  blink, and both available captures run FREE PLAY (no blinking coin
  text).
- NEW best candidate, method F: the stage-clear bonus tally ticks its
  counter at a fixed frames-per-tick rate with a tick sound per step.
  Tick rate in the AUDIO track = f_native / k_tick, k_tick measurable
  exactly in simulation; audio timing is precise to samples, both
  videos contain seven stage clears each, and tallies never slow down.
  This supersedes E as the primary, with C anchoring the chain clock.
